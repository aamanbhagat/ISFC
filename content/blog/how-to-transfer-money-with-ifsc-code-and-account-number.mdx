## Can You Transfer Money With IFSC Code and Account Number?

Yes — for any domestic Indian rupee transfer, the two pieces of information that actually move the money are the recipient's bank account number and the IFSC code of their bank branch. Everything else (name, branch address, mobile number) is mostly for verification and fraud checks.

The IFSC (Indian Financial System Code) is an 11-character alphanumeric code assigned by the Reserve Bank of India to every bank branch that participates in electronic payment systems. The structure is fixed:

- First 4 characters: bank code (e.g., SBIN for State Bank of India, HDFC for HDFC Bank, ICIC for ICICI Bank)
- 5th character: always 0, reserved for future use
- Last 6 characters: branch code, usually alphanumeric

So when you transfer money with IFSC code and account number, the IFSC tells the payment system exactly which branch holds the recipient's account, and the account number identifies the customer at that branch.

In practice, you usually cannot just type an account number and IFSC into your banking app and hit send. Almost every Indian bank requires you to first add the recipient as a "beneficiary" — a one-time registration that links the account number and IFSC to a nickname in your account. This is a fraud-control measure mandated by RBI guidelines and enforced by individual banks. Once the beneficiary is active, you can send money using IFSC code and account number as many times as you want through NEFT, RTGS, or IMPS.

A handful of banks and UPI apps allow one-time transfers without beneficiary registration, but with lower limits and additional OTP verification.

## What Other Details You May Need for the Transfer

The IFSC code and account number are mandatory, but your bank's net banking or mobile app will usually ask for a few more fields when you add a beneficiary:

- **Beneficiary name**: Should match the name on the recipient's bank account. Banks use this for name-match verification, but the actual credit is driven by account number and IFSC.
- **Bank name**: Auto-fills once you enter a valid IFSC, but useful as a sanity check.
- **Branch name and address**: Optional in most apps, but useful if you are unsure of the IFSC. You can [find the IFSC code by bank branch name](/blog/how-to-find-ifsc-code-by-bank-branch-name) instead of guessing.
- **Account type**: Savings, current, or NRE/NRO. Most domestic transfers don't strictly need this, but some banks ask.
- **Mobile number or email**: Used by some banks to send a confirmation to the beneficiary, not for the transfer itself.
- **Nickname**: For your own reference, e.g., "Mom" or "Ramesh Freelance".

For international inward transfers, the sender will also need the SWIFT/BIC code of the recipient's branch, which is a separate 8- or 11-character international identifier — not the same as IFSC. MICR code, the 9-digit magnetic ink code printed on cheques, is now largely legacy and not required for NEFT, RTGS, or IMPS initiated online.

## How to Transfer via NEFT Using IFSC Code

NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer) is RBI's batched payment system. As of 2026, NEFT is available 24x7, including weekends and RBI holidays, with half-hourly settlement batches. There is no minimum or maximum amount for NEFT — you can send as little as ₹1.

Here is the step-by-step process for a NEFT transfer using IFSC code:

1. Log in to your bank's net banking portal or mobile banking app.
2. Go to the "Fund Transfer" or "Payments" section and select "Add Beneficiary" or "Add Payee".
3. Choose "Bank Account" as the beneficiary type.
4. Enter the beneficiary name, account number, and IFSC code. The app will auto-fetch the bank and branch name — verify it.
5. Submit and authenticate with OTP or your profile password.
6. Wait for beneficiary activation. Most banks activate within 30 minutes in 2026; some take up to 4 hours. A few banks (HDFC, ICICI, Axis) offer instant activation with a capped first transfer.
7. Once active, go back to "Fund Transfer", select the beneficiary, choose "NEFT" as the mode, enter the amount and an optional remark.
8. Confirm with OTP, grid card details, or your transaction password.
9. The money is debited immediately and credited to the recipient in the next half-hourly settlement batch.

NEFT transfers are free for savings account customers at most banks — RBI waived NEFT charges in 2019 and that continues in 2026, though a few banks still levy a small fee for high-value transfers above ₹2 lakh on current accounts.

## How to Transfer via RTGS Using IFSC Code

RTGS (Real Time Gross Settlement) is RBI's high-value payment system where each transaction is settled individually and immediately, rather than in batches. As of 2026, RTGS is available 24x7x365, including holidays.

The minimum amount for an RTGS transfer with IFSC code is ₹2 lakh. There is no upper cap for online banking, though your bank may impose its own daily limit based on your account type and transaction rights.

Steps for an RTGS transfer:

1. Log in to net banking or your mobile app.
2. Add the beneficiary exactly as you would for NEFT — same account number and IFSC fields. The beneficiary is shared across NEFT, RTGS, and IMPS in most banks, so you only add once.
3. Wait for activation (same window as NEFT).
4. Select the beneficiary, choose "RTGS" as the mode.
5. Enter an amount of ₹2 lakh or more.
6. Confirm with your second-factor authentication.
7. The credit hits the recipient's account in seconds to a few minutes, since RTGS settles transaction-by-transaction.

RBI has waived RTGS processing charges for online originators since 2019. Your bank may still charge a small fee (typically ₹24.50 plus GST for amounts between ₹2 lakh and ₹5 lakh, ₹49.50 plus GST above ₹5 lakh) — these are bank-side charges, not RBI charges.

RTGS is the right choice when you need to send money using IFSC code for amounts above ₹2 lakh and need guaranteed same-day, often same-minute, settlement. For anything under ₹2 lakh, NEFT or IMPS is the better fit.

## How to Transfer via IMPS Using IFSC Code

IMPS (Immediate Payment Service) is run by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) and is the fastest of the three bank-account-based transfer methods. It is available 24x7, including Sundays and bank holidays, and credits are instantaneous.

There are two IMPS modes that use IFSC and account number:

- **IMPS P2A (Person-to-Account)**: Uses account number plus IFSC. This is the equivalent of NEFT or RTGS but instant.
- **IMPS P2P (Person-to-Person)**: Uses mobile number plus MMID, no IFSC needed. Largely legacy now.

The IMPS transfer with account number process:

1. Add the beneficiary in your banking app with account number and IFSC (same as NEFT or RTGS).
2. Once activated, select the beneficiary, choose "IMPS" as the mode.
3. Enter the amount and remark.
4. Authenticate.
5. The recipient's account is credited within seconds, usually under 30 seconds.

The IMPS limit is ₹5 lakh per transaction as of 2026 (raised by NPCI from the earlier ₹2 lakh cap). Your bank's daily IMPS limit may be lower — typically ₹50,000 to ₹2 lakh for savings accounts unless you have explicitly raised it.

IMPS charges: most banks charge ₹2.50 plus GST for amounts up to ₹1 lakh, ₹5 plus GST for amounts between ₹1 lakh and ₹2 lakh, and ₹15 plus GST above ₹2 lakh. Many private banks waive these for salary and premium account customers.

IMPS is the best option when you want an online bank transfer with IFSC code and account number and need the funds to land in under a minute, regardless of the time of day or whether it is a holiday.

A few 2026-specific notes:

- RBI's 24x7 NEFT and RTGS windows have been stable since 2019 and 2020 respectively, and there is no indication of any change in FY 2026-27.
- NPCI raised the IMPS per-transaction cap to ₹5 lakh in 2023 and it remains at ₹5 lakh in 2026.
- Most banks impose a daily aggregate transfer limit (₹10 lakh to ₹50 lakh for savings accounts) regardless of which system you use. You can request a higher limit by visiting your branch or through your net banking's "Transaction Rights" section.
- For current accounts, banks typically allow ₹25 lakh to ₹100 lakh per day, depending on the account variant.

## Common Mistakes When Entering IFSC Code for Transfer

Most failed or delayed transfers come from a small set of avoidable errors:

- **Wrong branch IFSC**: The recipient may have given you the IFSC of their bank's main branch instead of the branch where their account is actually held. Always confirm by asking the recipient to check the IFSC printed on their chequebook, passbook, or bank statement. You can also [find the IFSC code by bank branch name](/blog/how-to-find-ifsc-code-by-bank-branch-name) to cross-verify.
